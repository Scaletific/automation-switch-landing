WHY MOST AUTOMATION PROJECTS STALL AT STEP THREE

AS
Automation Switch
Mar 2026
7 min read
Workflow Architecture

The failure isn't in the tools. It's in how teams think about handoffs. Most automation stalls not because the technology is wrong — but because no one mapped what happens when the workflow hits a human decision point.

THE PROBLEM WITH STEP THREE

Every automation project starts with the same optimism. Step one works perfectly — data comes in, a trigger fires, something moves. Step two adds a transformation layer and that works too. Then step three arrives and suddenly everything that seemed clean becomes complicated.

Step three is usually where a human has to make a decision. Approve or reject. Assign or escalate. The automation reaches a branch point and the tooling has no way to hold that context while it waits.

The workflow isn't broken. The assumption that everything can be automated end-to-end is what breaks.

DESIGNING FOR HANDOFFS

The teams that succeed don't try to automate the human decision out of the process. They design the automation to carry context cleanly up to the handoff point, pause there, and resume cleanly afterwards. That requires thinking about state — what information does the human need, and how does the system hold everything while it waits?

The context package

Before any handoff, your automation should compile what we call a context package — a structured summary of everything that's happened so far, what decision needs to be made, and what the downstream steps will look like depending on the choice. The human shouldn't have to dig through logs or retrace steps.

// Example context package shape
{
  trigger: 'new_lead_submitted',
  lead: { name, company, industry, source },
  enrichment: { painPoints, stakeholderRole, intent },
  decision_required: 'approve_for_outreach',
  deadline: '2026-03-24T09:00:00Z',
  next_steps: {
    approved: 'generate_email_sequence',
    rejected: 'archive_and_tag'
  }
}

WHERE PRECISION REACH FITS

This is exactly the problem Precision Reach was built to solve for the outreach workflow specifically. By the time a lead reaches an SDR, the research is done, the pain points are surfaced, and the email sequence is already drafted. The human decision — whether to send — is the only thing left. The automation doesn't try to make that call. It just makes sure the human has everything they need to make it fast.
